# UTC Module 1 – Addiction as a Disease

**Programme:** Outpatient Treatment Curriculum
**NQF Levels:** 4 and 5
**HWSETA Unit Standards:** US 117151, US 259577
**Duration:** 6 contact hours
**Delivery Format:** Facilitated group session (3 × 2-hour sessions)

---

## Purpose

This module establishes the neurobiological and biopsychosocial foundation for all subsequent UTC modules. Learners develop a medically accurate, non-stigmatising understanding of addiction and apply this to their language, attitudes, and practice as outpatient treatment counsellors.

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, learners will be able to:

1. Describe the neurobiological basis of addiction, including the brain reward pathway and the role of dopamine dysregulation.
2. Explain the biopsychosocial model of SUD, integrating biological, psychological, and social/environmental risk factors.
3. Apply rights-based, person-first language in all clinical and community interactions.
4. Identify individual, family, and community-level risk and protective factors for SUDs.
5. Describe the South African epidemiological context of substance use disorders.
6. Apply PAIA and POPIA requirements for confidentiality in SUD treatment settings.

## Key Concepts

- **Neuroplasticity and chronic brain disorder model** — addiction changes brain structure and function; recovery is a process of neurological and behavioural rehabilitation.
- **DSM-5 / ICD-11 diagnostic criteria for Substance Use Disorders** — mild, moderate, severe spectrum.
- **Biopsychosocial model** — no single cause; interaction of genetic vulnerability, psychological factors (trauma, mental health), and social determinants (poverty, community norms).
- **Risk factors in the South African context** — GBV exposure, unemployment, intergenerational trauma, alcohol and drug availability, and cultural norms around substance use.
- **Protective factors** — family connectedness, school engagement, community cohesion, access to mental health services.
- **Person-first language** — "person with a substance use disorder" rather than "addict" or "junkie"; non-stigmatising framing in all documentation.
